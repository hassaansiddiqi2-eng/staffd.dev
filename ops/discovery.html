STAFFD.DEV · SALES PLAYBOOK

DISCOVERY
CALL SCRIPT

The exact 20-minute script for warm intro calls with US seed-stage founders. Every word, every pause, every pivot. Do not deviate on your first 10 calls. Once you have pattern recognition, adapt freely.

DURATION: 20 MINUTES
SPEAKER: CEO
FORMAT: ZOOM / GOOGLE MEET
GOAL: QUALIFY + BOOK TRIAL
1
OPENING & CREDIBILITY
MINUTES 0–3
SAY
"Hey [Name], good to meet you — thanks for making time. I know founders at your stage have zero of it, so I'll keep this tight. 20 minutes, I promise.

Quick context on me: I'm [Your Name], I run Staffd — we place pre-vetted React and Node.js engineers into US seed-stage teams. Usually within 7 days. We started because I kept seeing founders in my network burn 6–8 weeks and $20k on hiring that went nowhere.

But I don't want to pitch you — I want to understand if we're even a fit. Can I ask you a few questions first?"
NOTE
Why this works: You acknowledge their time scarcity, establish credibility in one sentence, and immediately hand control to them. Founders distrust pitches. Questions build trust. The phrase "I don't want to pitch you" is disarming — it signals you're not desperate.
NOTE
Camera on, background clean, no slides yet. This is a conversation, not a presentation. Save the deck for if they ask "what do you do exactly." Most won't — your intro covers it.
2
DISCOVERY — THEIR SITUATION
MINUTES 3–10
ASK
Q1 — The open: "So where are you with engineering hiring right now? Are you actively trying to fill something?"
LISTEN
Let them talk for 60–90 seconds. Don't interrupt. Nod. You're listening for: how long they've been stuck, what stack, how urgent it feels. The founder who says "we've had this open for 8 weeks" is ready to buy. The one who says "we're thinking about it for Q3" is not your customer today.
ASK
Q2 — The pain: "What's that costing you right now — like, what's actually not getting shipped because this role is open?"
NOTE
This question does the most work. You want them to say the cost out loud. The moment a founder says "we can't ship our payments feature" or "our competitor just launched what we were supposed to build" — their pain is activated. You didn't manufacture it, they said it. Remember the exact phrase they use. You'll repeat it back to them in the close.
ASK
Q3 — Previous attempts: "Have you tried anything else — recruiters, Upwork, anything? What happened?"
LISTEN
Most will have a bad experience story. Do not bash the previous provider. Just say "yeah, that's exactly what we hear — the process is broken, not the talent." This validates their frustration without being petty.
ASK
Q4 — The stack: "What does your current stack look like? And what specifically does this hire need to own?"
NOTE
You need: frontend/backend split, React version, Node or other backend, any specific frameworks (Next.js, tRPC, Prisma etc.), and whether they want someone who owns a feature or integrates into an existing team. Write this down. You'll use it when you describe the match.
ASK
Q5 — Timeline urgency: "If you could have the right engineer start next week — is that too soon, or is that exactly what you need?"
LISTEN
"That would be incredible" = hot lead. Move to pitch.
"We're probably 3–4 weeks out" = warm. Qualify timeline before pitching.
"We haven't budgeted for this yet" = not ready. Don't pitch. Ask what would need to be true for them to move. Schedule a follow-up for when budget unlocks.
3
THE PITCH — ONLY IF QUALIFIED
MINUTES 10–15
NOTE
Only pitch if they have an active open role, budget, and urgency. If any of these are missing, don't pitch — ask what would need to change. A bad-fit client wastes 3x more time than a clean "no."
SAY
"Okay, so based on what you just described — you need [REPEAT THEIR EXACT WORDS about the role and the pain] — here's exactly how we'd solve that.

We have a bench of pre-vetted [React/Node] engineers. Every single one has been through four stages: GitHub audit, a communication interview — that one kills more people than the coding test — a technical assessment, and a live coding session. We reject 97 out of every 100 who apply.

Here's what happens: I'd send you 2–3 profiles today — not CVs, actual profiles with GitHub links and a recorded technical session. You pick one to meet. That call is 45 minutes. If you like them, they can start this week.

And here's the part that makes this a no-brainer: you don't pay us anything for the first 14 days. Work with them, see what they build. If it's not right — for any reason — you walk away. No invoice."
NOTE
Pause after "No invoice." Don't fill the silence. Let them respond. The 14-day free trial is the offer. Give it room to land. Founders who are ready will say "okay, how does that work?" Founders who aren't will raise an objection — go to Phase 4.
SAY
"And if somehow — after the trial, after trying a replacement if needed — we still haven't placed someone shipping code in your codebase within 60 days, you don't owe us anything at all. We've never had to honour that. But it exists because you deserve to know what happens in the worst case."
4
OBJECTION HANDLING
MINUTES 13–17 (IF NEEDED)
OBJECTION
"I've been burned by offshore developers before."
+
"I'd be surprised if you hadn't — it's the most common thing we hear. And usually the problem isn't the talent, it's the process. You get dumped with a pile of profiles, you do all the screening yourself, and you're hoping whoever you pick can actually communicate in an async environment with a US team. That's a broken process.

We flip it. We vet before you see them. The communication interview alone eliminates more people than the technical test. And if it's still not right after you've started — we replace them. For free. No questions. The risk is ours."
Then: "Would it help to see what the profile pack looks like? I can show you a sample right now."
OBJECTION
"What about time zones? Pakistan is far."
+
"Totally fair question. Pakistan is UTC+5, so there's a gap — but we build in 4 to 6 hours of overlap with US East Coast hours as a standard requirement. And honestly, most seed-stage teams I work with are already async-first by necessity — your engineers aren't all in the same room anyway.

That said, tell me how your team actually works — are you doing daily standups, or is it more async Slack updates? We'd confirm the overlap setup on the intake call and make sure it works for your workflow before anyone starts."
Never be defensive about timezone. Turn it into a question about how they actually work — most seed teams are more async than they think.
OBJECTION
"How do I know their English is good enough?"
+
"Stage 2 of our vetting process is a 45-minute live call that is exclusively about communication. Not technical at all. We're testing: can they explain a decision clearly? Can they write an async update that unblocks a situation? Can they push back professionally on a bad idea?

More engineers fail that stage than fail the coding test. We're extremely serious about this — because we know a technically great engineer who communicates poorly is a nightmare for a US remote team. You'll see the communication interview is on the profile we send you."
Optional: "I can show you an example of the async writing test we use — it'll make it concrete."
OBJECTION
"We're not quite ready — maybe in a few weeks."
+
"That's totally fine — what's the blocker? Is it budget approval, a technical decision you're waiting on, something else?"
Then listen. If it's budget: "Understood — when does that unlock? I'll follow up then." If it's a decision: "What would need to be resolved for you to feel ready?" If they can't articulate it: they may just not be motivated enough yet. Do not push. Schedule a 15-min follow-up in 3 weeks and send a brief case study in the meantime.
OBJECTION
"What's the cost? / Are you expensive?"
+
"Depends on the level you need — but for a senior React/Node engineer, you're looking at $6,000 to $8,000 a month, all in. No recruiter fee on top. No hidden costs. The US equivalent for the same experience level is $12,000 to $18,000 a month — so you're typically saving 40 to 60%.

And remember — the first 14 days you're not paying anything. So your first bill doesn't come until you've already decided it's working."
Don't apologise for the price. State it plainly, anchor to the US equivalent, and remind them of the free trial. The free trial neutralises price anxiety more than any discount would.
OBJECTION
"I need to talk to my co-founder / technical lead first."
+
"Of course — makes total sense. Would it be easier to get them on a 15-minute call together? I can walk through the vetting process and answer their technical questions directly — saves you having to relay everything. When's a time that works for both of you?"
Always offer to get the decision-maker on the next call rather than going through a messenger. A three-way call closes faster than two sequential calls.
5
QUALIFICATION CHECKPOINT
MINUTE 16–17
NOTE
Before closing, quickly confirm all four qualification boxes are ticked. If any are missing — do not close. Re-qualify instead.
ASK
Mental checklist — do not ask these as explicit questions, confirm through conversation:

1. Active open role right now? (not "thinking about it") ✓
2. Budget exists and is approved? (not "we'll figure it out") ✓
3. React / Node.js stack? (or adjacent — confirm on intake call) ✓
4. Decision-maker is on this call? (or getting them on next call) ✓
6
THE CLOSE
MINUTES 17–20
NOTE
Use one of the three closes below based on their temperature. Never use all three. Pick one and commit to it.
CLOSE A — HOT LEAD (they've said yes to everything)
"Okay, here's what I'd suggest: let me send you 2–3 profiles today — React/Node engineers who are bench-ready right now, matched to [their stack they told you]. Take a look, pick one you want to meet, and we'll set up that 45-minute call. If you like them, they can start as early as [day of this week + 5 days]. Does that work?"
Then stop talking. The worst thing you can do after a close is fill the silence. Wait. If they say yes — confirm their email and send profiles within 2 hours. If they hesitate — go to a softer ask.
CLOSE B — WARM LEAD (interested but cautious)
"What would make the most sense as a next step for you? I could send profiles now and you look when you have time, or we could set up a quick intake call with your technical lead first to make sure the match is right — whatever gives you the most confidence."
Give them two options, both of which move forward. Never give them "think about it" as an option — it's a polite no. If they take either option, you're progressing.
CLOSE C — COOL LEAD (not ready but worth keeping)
"Makes total sense — you need [the thing they said needs to happen first]. Here's what I'll do: I'll send you a one-page summary of how the vetting process works and a sample profile so you can see exactly what you'd be evaluating. And let's put something in the calendar for [3 weeks from now] — even a 10-minute check-in. By then you'll probably have more clarity on [their blocker]."
Always leave a cool lead with (a) something concrete sent to their inbox and (b) a calendar hold. "Stay in touch" gets forgotten. A calendar hold doesn't.
CALL CHEAT SHEET
POWER PHRASES
"I don't want to pitch you — can I ask a few questions first?"
"What's actually not getting shipped because this role is open?"
"The risk is ours, not yours."
"You don't pay us anything for the first 14 days."
"We reject 97 out of every 100 who apply."
"More engineers fail the communication test than the coding test."
PHRASES TO AVOID
"We're based in Pakistan" — not on the first call. Lead with quality, reveal location after trust is built.
"Offshore / outsource / remote team" — these words activate the wrong mental model.
"Does that make sense?" — signals insecurity. Replace with silence.
"We can definitely do that" — before you've confirmed bench availability.
"Let me know if you're interested" — too passive. Always offer a next step.
